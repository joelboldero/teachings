Hearing God's Voice

Summary & Roadmap — Young Adults Session

Conviction God is not distant or silent — He desires to reveal Himself and lead His people.
Confidence Every follower of Jesus can grow in recognising the Shepherd's voice through relationship with Him.
Wisdom We can discern what God is saying, stay grounded in Scripture, and respond in obedience.

The Big Idea

  • Hearing God's voice is not reserved for a spiritual elite — it is the normal experience of belonging to Jesus.
  • It is not primarily a skill to be mastered but a relationship to be cultivated.
  • The central issue has never simply been whether God speaks — it has always been which voice we choose to trust.
  • God speaks to reveal Himself, accomplish His purposes, and draw His people into deeper relationship with Him.
1

Does God Speak? — The Biblical Foundation

"My sheep hear my voice; I know them, and they follow me." — John 10:27

  • The question is not "Can I hear God?" but "Is God the kind of God who speaks?"
  • God speaks at creation — Genesis 1 is structured around "And God said..." seven times. Speech is fundamental to His nature as a personal God who makes Himself known.
  • God speaks relationally — Adam and Eve recognised His presence in the garden (Genesis 3:8). Fellowship with God was the original design for humanity.
  • The first temptation targeted God's voice — "Did God really say...?" (Genesis 3:1). The enemy's strategy: undermine confidence in what God has spoken.
  • God pursued first — Even after the Fall, God asked "Where are you?" — an invitation, not a question. Divine initiative runs through the entire biblical story.
  • God speaks relentlessly — God called Noah, Abraham, Moses, the prophets — always before they responded. The phrase "the word of the LORD came..." runs through the whole Old Testament.
  • Moses' longing → Joel's promise → Pentecost's fulfilment
    • Moses: "I wish that all the LORD's people were prophets" (Numbers 11:29)
    • Joel: "I will pour out my Spirit on all people" (Joel 2:28–29)
    • Peter at Pentecost: "This is what was spoken by the prophet Joel" (Acts 2:16)
  • John 10:27 is the fulfilment of this entire story — hearing the Shepherd's voice is the normal consequence of belonging to Him.
2

How Does God Speak? — Through Scripture

"All Scripture is God-breathed..." — 2 Timothy 3:16

  • Scripture is the primary way God reveals Himself — the standard against which all other communication is tested.
  • God-breathed (theopneustos) — Scripture originates from the very breath of God, not merely human reflection about God.
  • Scripture is alive and active — Hebrews 4:12 uses the present tense deliberately. The Holy Spirit who inspired Scripture also illuminates it today.
  • The Pharisees warning (John 5:39–40) — it is possible to know the Bible without knowing the God of the Bible. The purpose of Scripture is to lead us to Jesus, not just information.
  • God's priority is transformation, not just guidance — we want to know what to do; God is more concerned with who we are becoming.
  • The Hebrew word shema — "hear" means to listen attentively, embrace, and obey. Hearing is never complete until it produces a response.
  • Key principle: we should not be surprised if we struggle to hear God's voice in the extraordinary while neglecting His voice in the ordinary.
  • Prayer and Bible reading belong together — reading without prayer gains information while missing transformation.
3

How Does God Speak? — Through the Holy Spirit

"He will glorify me because it is from me that He will receive what He will make known to you." — John 16:14

  • Jesus called His departure a gain — through the Holy Spirit, God would now dwell within every believer, not just among them.
  • The direction of redemption has always been toward greater intimacy: garden → tabernacle → temple → Christ among us → Spirit within us.
  • The Spirit's ministry is relational before it is revelatory — His purpose is to make Christ present, not to deliver supernatural information.
  • Key test: the Holy Spirit always glorifies Christ. If a supposed revelation draws attention away from Jesus, it does not originate from the Holy Spirit.
  • Scripture and the Spirit are not competitors — the Spirit inspired Scripture and never speaks independently of it.
  • Hearing the Holy Spirit is about walking closely enough with Him to become familiar with His leading, convictions, and gentle guidance.
4

How Does God Speak? — Through Gentle Promptings

"Your ears will hear a voice behind you, saying, 'This is the way; walk in it.'" — Isaiah 30:21

  • Dramatic encounters (burning bush, visions, audible voice) are the exception, not the norm. God most often leads through quiet, relational communication.
  • Elijah at Mount Horeb (1 Kings 19) — the key lesson:
    • Wind, earthquake, fire: God was not in any of them
    • After the drama: a "still small voice" — qol demamah daqqah, "the sound of thin silence"
    • God did not rebuke Elijah's fear — He provided rest, food, and water first
    • God asked questions to invite reflection, not because He lacked information
    • God gave the next steps, not a map of the entire future
  • Psalm 119:105 — "a lamp for my feet." A lamp in the ancient world lit only the next few steps, not the entire road ahead.
  • God's promptings often come as: a growing conviction, a quiet reminder of Scripture, an increasing sense of peace about a decision, or a gentle nudge that aligns with His character.
  • Discernment grows through relationship — the more we know God's character through Scripture, the more readily we recognise His leading.
5

How Does God Speak? — Dreams, Visions & Prophecy

"I will pour out my Spirit on all people. Your sons and daughters will prophesy..." — Joel 2:28

  • These are genuine means God has used — there is no biblical reason to conclude He cannot use them today.
  • They are acts of God's sovereign initiative, not methods believers can manufacture or demand.
  • Joseph (Genesis 37) — dreams provided direction, but character was formed through the waiting. Revelation does not always eliminate uncertainty.
  • Daniel (Daniel 8:27; 12:8) — even the most spiritually mature person may receive revelation they do not fully understand. Receiving revelation ≠ understanding revelation. Humility is essential.
  • Peter (Acts 10) and Paul (Acts 16:9) — visions in Acts always serve redemptive purposes, never personal fascination.
  • The goal is Christlikeness, not spiritual spectacle. Extraordinary experiences are evidence of the Spirit's presence — not ends in themselves.
  • Every dream, vision, or prophetic word must point toward Christ and be accountable to Scripture.
6

How Does God Speak? — People, Circumstances & Creation

"Plans fail for lack of counsel, but with many advisers they succeed." — Proverbs 15:22

  • Through people — Jethro with Moses, Nathan with David, Priscilla & Aquila with Apollos. God regularly works through human relationships. Seeking counsel is humility, not weakness.
  • Through circumstances — Paul in Acts 16: closed doors in Asia and Bithynia led him to Macedonia. Key principle: circumstances work alongside obedience, they don't replace discernment.
    • Open doors ≠ automatic green light (Jonah found an open ship while running from God)
    • Closed doors ≠ God saying no (Paul faced hardship while obeying God's call)
    • Better question: "How is God calling me to respond faithfully within these circumstances?"
  • Through creation — Psalm 19:1; Romans 1:20. Creation points us to a Creator who is great. But it cannot reveal the gospel — for that we need Scripture.
  • The integrated picture: Scripture forms the foundation → the Spirit illuminates → promptings lead in daily obedience → wise believers confirm and correct → circumstances are woven together by providence → creation reminds us of His greatness. None of these stand alone. They work together.
7

Recognising the Shepherd's Voice

"His sheep follow him because they know his voice." — John 10:4

  • Recognition grows out of relationship, not technique. Sheep recognise their shepherd because they have spent time with him, not because they mastered voice identification.
  • "I know them" comes before "they follow me" — our security rests in the Shepherd's faithfulness, not our flawless hearing.
  • Recognition grows through obedience — "Anyone who chooses to do the will of God will find out whether my teaching comes from God" (John 7:17). Obedience sharpens spiritual perception.
  • Samuel (1 Samuel 3) — could not recognise God's voice at first, not because of rebellion but because of inexperience. God called him again and again patiently. Recognition is cultivated over time.
  • Jesus chose the image of sheep, not students. Students are recognised by what they know. Sheep are recognised by whom they follow.
  • The closer we walk with the Shepherd, the more familiar His voice becomes. Over time, what once seemed uncertain becomes recognisable because familiarity has replaced fear.
8

Discerning the Shepherd's Voice

"Test everything; hold fast what is good." — 1 Thessalonians 5:21

  • Discernment is not unbelief — it is humility. God speaks perfectly; we do not always hear perfectly.
  • Safeguard 1 — Scripture: God's voice will never contradict God's Word. If an impression opposes Scripture, no further discernment is needed — Scripture has already spoken. (Isaiah 8:20; Acts 17:11)
  • Safeguard 2 — The character of Christ: The Spirit always glorifies Jesus (John 16:14). Ask: does this reflect His humility, holiness, compassion, and truth? Does it produce the fruit of the Spirit? (Galatians 5:22–23)
    • Conviction vs condemnation: conviction says "come back to your Father" — condemnation says "you have failed and there is no hope" (Romans 8:1)
  • Safeguard 3 — Christian community: Antioch church (Acts 13:1–3) and Jerusalem Council (Acts 15) both discerned together. Trusted pastors, mentors, and mature friends see what we miss.
  • Safeguard 4 — Time: God is rarely in a hurry. Waiting exposes motives and clarifies direction. Genuine guidance is seldom characterised by panic or unnecessary urgency.
  • Safeguard 5 — Peace: "Let the peace of Christ rule in your hearts" (Colossians 3:15) — brabeuō, to act as an umpire. Peace is a confirming witness alongside other safeguards, not a standalone basis for decisions.
  • Our confidence rests not in perfect discernment but in the faithfulness of the Shepherd who does not abandon His sheep.
9

What If I Get It Wrong?

"Now Samuel did not yet know the LORD: The word of the LORD had not yet been revealed to him." — 1 Samuel 3:7

  • Fear of making a mistake can become one of the greatest obstacles to spiritual growth.
  • The Bible never presents mature believers as people who never misunderstand God — it presents them as people who remain teachable.
  • Nathan (2 Samuel 7) — a faithful prophet who initially gave David the wrong counsel. God corrected him and continued to use him. Spiritual maturity means being humble enough to receive correction.
  • Peter (Matthew 16) — extraordinary insight and profound misunderstanding in the same conversation. Jesus kept shaping him anyway.
  • Agabus (Acts 21) — the broad direction of his prophecy was true; some details unfolded differently. God's revelation is perfect; our interpretation remains finite.
  • Learning to hear God is not a spiritual exam we pass or fail. It is a lifelong journey of discipleship marked by humble dependence, not flawless discernment.
  • Faith is not the absence of uncertainty — it is confidence in the goodness of the One who is leading us.
10

Listening Prayer — Two-Way Journaling

"Be still, and know that I am God." — Psalm 46:10

  • Two-Way Journaling is not a formula for making God speak. It is a way of slowing down, creating space to listen, and recording what the Holy Spirit may be impressing on your heart so it can later be tested against Scripture.
  • Step 1 — Stillness: find a quiet place; put your phone away; write down distracting tasks to clear your mind; confess any sin honestly before God (1 John 1:9).
  • Step 2 — Prayer: simply ask the Holy Spirit to illuminate your mind and help you recognise God's voice. You are not persuading God to speak — He already delights in communicating with His children.
  • Step 3 — Fix your attention on Jesus: read a short Scripture passage about Christ, or quietly worship. The goal is to direct your thoughts toward the One whose voice you desire to hear.
  • Step 4 — Write honestly: speak to God in your journal like a trusted friend. Record your questions, concerns, hopes, fears, and decisions. Be completely honest.
  • Step 5 — Listen and record: quietly wait. Pay attention to the gentle flow of thoughts, Scriptures, impressions, or images that arise while your attention is on Christ. Write them down without overanalysing — do not stop after every line to ask "Was that really God?" Allow the writing to flow.
  • Step 6 — Discernment: after writing, test everything against Scripture. Does it reflect Christ's character? Does it produce the fruit of the Spirit? Share significant entries with a trusted, mature believer for perspective.
  • The goal is not impressive spiritual experiences — it is relationship. Faithfulness is measured not by how much we hear but by how faithfully we respond to what God has already said.

The Roadmap in Brief

  • Foundation: God is a speaking God — this is who He is, from Genesis to Pentecost to today.
  • Primary channel: Scripture — alive, active, Christ-centred, tested by the Spirit who inspired it.
  • Ongoing channel: The Holy Spirit — relational, Christ-glorifying, always consistent with Scripture.
  • Everyday experience: Gentle promptings, wise counsel, providential circumstances, and creation — all integrated under God's sovereign hand.
  • Recognition: Grows through relationship, time, and obedience — not through technique.
  • Discernment: Scripture → Christ's character → community → time → peace. All five safeguards working together.
  • Freedom from fear: Mature believers are not people who never get it wrong — they are people who remain humble, teachable, and dependent on the Shepherd.
  • Practice: Two-Way Journaling — stillness, prayer, focus on Jesus, honest writing, listening, discernment.
  • The goal is never a technique. The goal is always the Shepherd.
